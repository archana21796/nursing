Home physiotherapy after knee replacement. A 12-week recovery guide.

What 12 weeks of post-knee-replacement home physiotherapy actually looks like in India — week by week.

By the CareStride team · Reviewed by Sree, founder & CEO · ~10 minute read · Published April 2026

Total knee replacement (TKR), known clinically as total knee arthroplasty, is one of India's most commonly performed orthopaedic surgeries. Recovery is highly dependent on consistent post-operative physiotherapy, and the first 12 weeks are when most of the functional gain happens. This guide covers what those 12 weeks typically look like when physiotherapy is delivered at home in India — what to expect each week, what targets to discuss with your physiotherapist, when to escalate concerns to your surgeon, and what families can do to support recovery between sessions.

Important note: This guide describes typical post-TKR recovery patterns. Every patient and surgery is different. Your surgeon's specific protocol, your pre-surgical fitness, your age and underlying conditions, and the type of implant used all affect what your recovery should actually look like. The week-by-week targets below are typical of an uncomplicated recovery, not a prescription. Your physiotherapist and surgeon set your specific targets — this guide helps you understand the conversation they're having.

Why home physiotherapy after TKR?

Three structural reasons home physiotherapy is well-suited to post-TKR recovery in India: (1) the patient typically can't drive for 4–6 weeks, making clinic visits logistically painful; (2) the patient is often in significant discomfort for the first 2–3 weeks, and travelling reluctance can result in skipped sessions, which directly worsens outcomes; (3) most of the work in the first 6 weeks is range-of-motion exercises, manual therapy, and home-environment-specific gait training — none of which require specialised equipment.

For specialised interventions (hydrotherapy, advanced electrotherapy, gym-based functional training), a clinic visit may be added later in recovery. The home physiotherapist coordinates this with the patient's surgeon and refers to a clinic when clinically indicated. Weeks 0–1: discharge to first week home

Most TKR patients in India are discharged 3–5 days after surgery, depending on the surgeon's protocol and the patient's recovery trajectory in hospital. By discharge, the patient should be: walking short distances with a walker, performing basic ankle pumps and quad sets in bed, sitting up and standing with support, and managing pain on prescribed medications. The discharge team will provide a written rehabilitation plan; bring this to your physiotherapist's first visit.

Typical home physiotherapy goals, weeks 0–1: Knee flexion (bending) of 70–90 degrees by end of week 1. Knee extension (straightening) close to 0 degrees. Walking with a walker for short indoor distances, multiple times daily. Independent transfers (bed to chair, chair to standing). Basic ice and elevation to manage swelling.

What home visits cover: The first home physiotherapy visit typically happens within 1–2 days of discharge. Initial assessment, pain and swelling management techniques (icing protocol, elevation, gentle mobilisation), supervised range-of-motion exercises, transfer technique review, and home environment safety (rugs, stair access, bathroom adaptations). Frequency in weeks 0–2 is typically daily or every other day.

Escalate to your surgeon if: uncontrolled pain not relieved by prescribed medications, new severe swelling beyond what's been gradually improving, fever above 38°C / 100.4°F, calf pain or unilateral leg swelling (potential DVT), wound discharge or persistent oozing, unable to bear partial weight by end of week 1.

Weeks 2–4: range of motion and walking confidence

This is the window where most range-of-motion gains happen. The knee is healing, swelling is decreasing, and the patient is becoming more confident with the walker. The risk in this window is the opposite of weeks 0–1: patients sometimes feel "good enough" and reduce their exercise frequency, which can result in stiffness that becomes very difficult to recover.

Typical home physiotherapy goals, weeks 2–4: Knee flexion of 90–110 degrees by end of week 4. Full knee extension (0 degrees, sometimes slight hyperextension). Walking with a walker indoors and outdoors for progressively longer distances. Beginning to transition from walker to a single cane (typically end of week 3 or 4, depending on surgeon's clearance). Climbing stairs one at a time using the railing.

What home visits cover: Progressive range-of-motion exercises (passive, active-assisted, active), strengthening exercises (quad sets, straight-leg raises, glute bridges, hamstring sets), stationary bike if available, gentle stretching, and continued gait training with focus on weight-bearing through the surgical side. Frequency typically reduces to 3–5 sessions per week.

Escalate to your surgeon if: knee flexion plateaus or is regressing, new instability or "giving way" sensation, persistent or new sharp pain (different from soreness from exercise), unable to walk with walker for at least 5 minutes by end of week 2.

Weeks 5–8: strength and functional gait

By week 5, most patients have achieved 110–120 degrees of flexion and have transitioned to a single cane or are walking unaided indoors. The focus shifts from "can the knee move" to "can the knee carry the patient through normal life." This is also when many patients want to do too much too quickly — trying to climb hills, lift grandchildren, or skip exercises. The physiotherapist's role at this stage is as much pacing as it is exercises.

Typical goals, weeks 5–8: Knee flexion of 115–125 degrees by end of week 8 (a typical clinical target is 120 degrees, although some patients exceed this and some take longer to reach it). Walking unaided indoors comfortably. Outdoor walking with a single cane for longer durations. Climbing stairs alternating feet (with railing). Returning to driving (with surgeon's clearance, typically at 6 weeks). Returning to most light household activities.

What home visits cover: Resistance band strengthening, balance training (single-leg stance, tandem walking, step-ups), endurance work, sport-specific or hobby-specific functional training (gardening, religious observance involving floor sitting if culturally relevant, etc.), and continued range-of-motion to push toward the patient's individual flexion target. Frequency drops to 2–3 sessions per week.

Weeks 9–12: return to full function

By week 12, most uncomplicated TKR patients are walking without aids, climbing stairs comfortably, and returning to most pre-surgical activities. Some recovery continues for 6–12 months — particularly the resolution of residual swelling and the strengthening of supporting muscles — but the formal post-surgical rehabilitation programme typically transitions to maintenance by week 12.

Typical goals, weeks 9–12: Knee flexion at the patient's individual target (commonly 120–130 degrees for daily living; some patients achieve more). Walking unaided for long durations. Stair climbing alternating feet without railing if comfortable. Returning to low-impact recreational activity (walking, cycling, swimming, golf). Discharge from formal physiotherapy with a maintenance home exercise programme.

What home visits cover: Sport- or activity-specific reconditioning, advanced strengthening, gait refinement, and a structured maintenance home exercise programme the patient can continue independently. Frequency typically drops to weekly or every other week before formal discharge from physiotherapy.

What families can do between sessions

Family involvement is one of the strongest predictors of post-TKR recovery quality, especially for elderly patients. The most useful things family members can do:

  • Encourage daily home exercises. The home physiotherapist demonstrates exercises during each visit; patients should do them several times a day between visits. Family encouragement (without nagging) makes a measurable difference.
  • Track range of motion weekly. A simple goniometer (or even a smartphone protractor app) can measure knee flexion. Tracking the trajectory helps both patient and physiotherapist see progress.
  • Manage the home environment. Remove rugs that can shift, ensure stair railings are secure, position frequently-used items at waist height to reduce bending, and clear walking paths.
  • Watch for warning signs. Family members are often the first to notice subtle changes — new asymmetric swelling, a different gait pattern, increased reliance on the unaffected leg. Report these to the physiotherapist or surgeon.
  • Pace, don't push. Encouraging the patient to do "one more rep" is rarely the limiting factor. Helping them not overdo activities on good days — which can result in setbacks — is often more useful.

Choosing the right physiotherapist

Post-TKR rehabilitation is a specialised area of physiotherapy. Look for a physiotherapist with: (1) verified credentials (BPT, MPT, or DPT degree, with state physiotherapy council registration), (2) demonstrated experience with post-orthopaedic-surgery patients, (3) the ability to coordinate with your surgeon (reading discharge summaries, understanding the surgical protocol), and (4) consistent availability for the 4–8 week intensive phase.

For a more general framework on choosing healthcare professionals to come to your home, the principles are similar to those covered in our companion guide on choosing a home nurse: how to choose a home nurse for elderly parents in India →. The verification, language match, and grievance process questions apply equally to physiotherapists.

The 12-week trajectory above is typical, not guaranteed. Some patients recover faster, some slower, some have complications that require modified protocols. Your surgeon's specific instructions and your physiotherapist's clinical judgement override anything in this guide.
